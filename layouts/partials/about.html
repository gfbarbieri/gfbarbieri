About

By day I am an economist at the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) and by night I'm a second-year Ph.D. student in the economics program at George Mason University.


At the BLS, I work to analyze and improve household surveys and data pipelines that determine where to collect the prices used to calculate the Consumer Price Index (CPI).. I have worked on projects to analyze survey metrics (response rates, non-response analysis), develop algorithms for record linkage and entity resolution, and web scraping. In fact, many of the code tutorials are derived from projects at the BLS.


Over the last year, I have been invited to lecture for the data science certificate program at Georgetown University's School of Continuing Studies. I lecture on using visual techniques in data pipelines, from exploratory data analysis to modeling, including feature analysis and model selection, steering, and evaluation.


As for academics, my research so far has focused on the economic processes that guide individuals to transform waste, and by-products generally, into useful goods. The economics of by-products has proved an exciting medium to explore microeconomics, economic history, market process theory, and entrepreneurship. Comparative statics and constrained optimization, the workhorses of economic theory, have proved useful for understanding current literature and deriving implcations. That said, simulations and networks, including agent-based modelling, have proved a useful technique for developing intuition about complex systems generally.
