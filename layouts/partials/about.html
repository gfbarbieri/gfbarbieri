About

By day I am an economist at the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) and by night I'm a second-year Ph.D. student in the economics program at George Mason University.


At the BLS, I work on teams that maintain, analyze, and improve the household surveys and data pipelines that determine which stores are visited to collect prices used to calculate the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Using SAS and Python software, I analyze survey metrics (response rates, non-response analysis), develop and maintain algorithms for record linkage and entity resolution, and ingest and wrangle data from multiple sources, including web scraping. In fact, many of the code tutorials are derived from projects at the BLS.


About three times a year I am invited to be a lecturer for the data science certificate program at Georgetown University's School of Continuing Studies. I lecture on using visual techniques to analyze data pipelines, from exploratory data analysis to modeling. Visual analytics in machine learning includes feature analysis, and model selection, steering, and evaluation.


As far as academics, the goal of my research is to study the economic processes that guide individuals to transform waste, and by-products generally, into useful goods. The economics of by-products is a medium through which I explore my interests in microeconomics, economic history, market process theory, and entrepreneurship. Mainstream economic methods, such as comparative statics and constrained optimization are the workhorses of economic theory. Despite the highly interconnected landscape that defines our society, I have found these techniques helpful. That said, I find simulations and networks a more useful modeling technique for developing intuition about complex systems generally, including agent-based models and other algorithmic methodologies.


From what I can tell, most of the applied research on by-products compares with virgin materials versus recycled materials, focuses on post-consumer waste, and emphasizes environment impacts. To my dismay, the bulk of the research is categorized under "environmental economics" or "natural resource economics". My goal is to broaden the concept of waste management and analyze how economic systems evolve to handle the production of by-products generally.
